Project Plan

Survey Allocation Optimisation

A constrained optimisation approach to minimising respondent cost across Tracksuit's monthly survey operations, while meeting qualification targets and demographic representation requirements.

Organisation Tracksuit
Role Data Scientist
Effort 5–10 hours
Stack Python · PuLP · Streamlit

Problem Statement

Tracksuit conducts surveys across 80 product categories monthly. Each category requires approximately 200 qualified respondents, where qualification is determined by probabilistic incidence rates unique to each category. Respondents have a maximum interview length of 8 minutes. The challenge is to design survey bundles — combinations of categories assigned to individual respondents — and allocate respondents across those bundles such that all qualification targets are met with minimum total respondents surveyed.

n
Category Targets
Every category must receive ≈200 qualified respondents per month (≥2,400/year). Required respondent pool varies by incidence rate: a category at 20% incidence requires ~1,000 exposures to yield 200 qualified.
t
Time Budget
Each respondent has a maximum of 480 seconds (8 minutes) of total interview time. Qualifier questions consume 0 seconds. Bundling multiple categories risks exceeding this limit if a respondent qualifies for several surveys simultaneously.
d
Demographic Representativeness
The pool of respondents exposed to each category's qualifier must match national population distributions across gender, age, and region. Qualification skew is acceptable; exposure skew is not.
Optimisation Objective

Minimise total respondents surveyed, subject to all three constraints above. The decision variables are the number of respondents allocated to each pre-defined survey bundle.

Staged Approach

1

Toy Problem & Manual Intuition

~1 hour

Build intuition by solving a scaled-down version manually before writing any code.

Approach

  • Scale the problem to 10 categories, 10 qualified target per category, ~120 respondents
  • Manually allocate respondents to single-category and multi-category bundles in a spreadsheet
  • Test bundle strategies: pairing high-incidence categories, pairing short surveys, mixing profiles
  • Track time utilisation per respondent and target coverage per category through manual iteration

Outcomes

  • Intuition for which pairing strategies produce efficient allocations
  • A manual baseline solution (total respondents needed for the toy problem)
  • Understanding of which constraints bind first (time vs. incidence vs. coverage)
2

Random Allocation Benchmark

~1 hour

Establish a naive baseline to quantify the value of optimisation.

Approach

  • Generate random bundles (singles, pairs, triples) that fit within the 8-minute budget
  • Allocate respondents uniformly across bundles
  • Simulate qualification outcomes using incidence rates as Bernoulli probabilities
  • Measure total respondents required, per-category coverage variance, and time waste

Outcomes

  • Benchmark metrics: total respondents needed, variance in target coverage, average time utilisation
  • Performance delta vs. manual toy solution to validate intuition
  • Quantified inefficiency of naive allocation as baseline for improvement
3

Bundle Generation & PuLP Optimisation

~2 hours

Design candidate bundles, formulate the LP, and solve for optimal allocation.

Bundle Generation

  • Enumerate all feasible single-category bundles (80 bundles)
  • Enumerate feasible size-2 bundles: all pairs where combined expected time ≤ 480 seconds
  • Enumerate feasible size-3 bundles: all triples where combined expected time ≤ 480 seconds
  • Pre-compute per bundle: expected time, expected qualified yield per category

Bundle Design Rationale

  • Bundles are intentionally constructed by pairing low-incidence categories with high-incidence categories to maximise expected qualified yield per respondent
  • A respondent assigned three low-incidence categories (e.g., 0.10, 0.15, 0.20) would have an expected yield of only 0.45 qualified responses — high risk of contributing nothing
  • Pairing a low-incidence category (0.10) with a high-incidence category (0.90) raises expected yield to 1.0 — ensuring almost every respondent qualifies for at least one survey
  • This strategy stabilises the distribution of expected yield across all respondents: the median and mean expected yield per respondent remain closely aligned, minimising outliers and ensuring efficient use of every recruited respondent
  • Real-world consideration: although qualifier questions are modelled as 0 seconds, bundle sizes are capped at 3 categories to respect practical limits on respondent burden and survey flow — strong alignment with real-world implementation constraints

PuLP Formulation

  • Decision variables: x_bundle for each candidate bundle (respondent count)
  • Objective: minimise Σ x_bundle across all bundles
  • Category constraints: for each category, Σ (incidence × x_bundle) across containing bundles ≥ 200
  • Time feasibility: implicit — infeasible bundles excluded at generation
  • Non-negativity: x_bundle ≥ 0

Outcomes

  • Optimal bundle set with respondent allocations per bundle
  • Total respondents required (optimised solution)
  • Expected qualified respondents per category verified against targets
4

Simulation & Validation

~1.5 hours

Validate the optimised allocation under probabilistic conditions via Monte Carlo simulation.

Respondent Generation

  • Generate synthetic respondent pool stratified by NZ census proportions
  • Gender: quota-fill to census ratio (e.g., 51% female, 49% male)
  • Age: 6 brackets matched to national distribution
  • Region: proportional to population density

Simulation

  • Assign respondents to bundles per PuLP allocation
  • For each respondent, draw qualification per category (Bernoulli with p = incidence rate)
  • Compute actual time spent (sum of survey lengths for qualified categories)
  • Aggregate: qualified count per category, time budget violations, demographic distribution
  • Run N iterations (e.g., 1,000) to build confidence intervals on target achievement

Outcomes

  • Simulated vs. expected qualified per category with confidence intervals
  • Percentage of respondents exceeding 8-minute budget (target: <1%)
  • Demographic representativeness check across all category exposure pools
  • Expected yield stability: verify that the median and mean expected yield per respondent are closely aligned across the respondent pool — a wide gap indicates imbalanced bundling where some respondents are underutilised while others are overloaded
5

Comparative Analysis & Visualisation

~1 hour

Compare strategies, quantify efficiency gains, and present results on a Streamlit dashboard.

Comparisons

  • Random allocation vs. optimised (singles only) vs. optimised (pairs) vs. optimised (triples)
  • Metrics: total respondents, target coverage reliability, time utilisation efficiency

Visualisations

  • Bar plot: total respondents by strategy (random vs. optimised variants)
  • Heatmap: qualified respondents per category (expected vs. simulated)
  • Distribution plot: time spent per respondent (CDF across strategies)
  • Expected yield distribution: histogram of expected yield per respondent with median and mean annotated — confirms bundling strategy produces balanced respondent utilisation
  • Stacked bar: allocation breakdown by bundle type (singles, pairs, triples)
  • Demographic validation: exposed pool distributions vs. census targets

Outcomes

  • Quantified efficiency gain (e.g., "optimised uses X% fewer respondents")
  • Visual proof of constraint satisfaction across all categories
  • Insight into which bundle strategies drove the most value
6

Documentation & Delivery

~30 min

Package findings, document assumptions, and prepare for technical discussion.

Deliverables

  • Reproducible codebase: bundle generation, PuLP solver, simulation, visualisation as modular functions
  • Assumptions documented: uniform incidence rates across demographics, census-based stratification, independence of category qualification
  • Limitations identified: linear relaxation vs. integer constraints, independence assumption, real-world qualifier burden
  • Extension recommendations: demographic-specific incidence rates, dynamic mid-month reallocation, qualifier time modelling

Outcomes

  • Clean, commented, reproducible code ready for review
  • Executive summary of approach and results
  • Prepared talking points for technical interview discussion
